EU AI Act for Companies: Which Risk Class Applies to Your System — and What's Due by When?

Short answer

The AI Act doesn't classify AI systems by model, but by purpose: the same base model can be minimal-risk in one use case and high-risk in the next. Four classes determine the obligations: prohibited, high-risk, limited risk, minimal risk. The mistake that costs companies the most: AI-powered recruiting counts as high-risk almost always — even if it just feels like an add-on feature in the applicant-tracking software.

What four risk classes does the AI Act distinguish?

Classification depends on the purpose, not the model: the same foundation model can be minimal-risk in one use case and high-risk in the next. The class is therefore a property of the specific use case — not of the underlying technology. Most enterprise use cases land in the two middle classes.

Prohibited

Art. 5 — since February 2025

Social scoring, manipulative systems, real-time biometric surveillance in public spaces. Absolute ban, no exceptions.

High-risk

Annex III

Application screening, performance evaluation, credit scoring, predictive policing. Conformity assessment, documentation, human oversight, registration.

Limited risk

Art. 50 — labeling obligation

Chatbots, AI-generated content, emotion recognition. Users must know they're interacting with AI.

Minimal risk

The majority of all systems

AI-powered search, spam filters, recommendation systems, simple automation. No AI Act obligations, but GDPR still applies.

By when does my system have to meet the requirements?

Deadlines are staggered, not tied to one cutoff date: bans and transparency duties have long applied, general-purpose-AI obligations since August 2025. The big shift concerns high-risk systems under Annex III — their obligations were pushed back 16 months to December 2027 by the Digital Omnibus, while labeling and transparency duties still kick in earlier, unchanged.

02/02/2025

Prohibited practices in force

Art. 5 (social scoring, manipulative systems, etc.) and AI literacy obligations (Art. 4) have applied since then.

08/02/2025

Obligations for general-purpose AI

Foundation model providers, governance structures, and fine regimes kick in.

08/02/2026

Transparency & registration obligations

General information and registration obligations for AI systems become applicable.

12/02/2026

Labeling obligation for AI content

Art. 50(2) (labeling AI-generated content) becomes applicable.

12/02/2027

High-risk systems (Annex III)postponed

New deadline via the Digital Omnibus (agreement 05/07/2026) — 16 months later than originally planned. Substantive obligations (Art. 6–27) unchanged.

08/02/2028

High-risk as a safety component (Annex I)

AI as a safety component in already-regulated products (e.g., machinery, medical devices) — its own, later deadline.

What did the Digital Omnibus actually change?

The Digital Omnibus shifts only the deadlines, not the substantive requirements. Reason: the harmonized standards companies are meant to use as a benchmark weren't available before the end of 2026 — the EU didn't want to bring an obligation into force without a reliable testing standard, but deliberately decided against softening the content itself.

Important here: no framework replaces another. GDPR, the voluntary GPAI Code of Practice (with “safe harbor” effect), and the ISO/IEC 42001 certification standard all apply in parallel and cumulatively alongside the AI Act — anyone looking at only one deadline misses the other three. Anyone still waiting in 2026 also gives up lead time: risk management and documentation can be built up independently of the exact cutoff date.

Anti-pattern

The misconception I encounter most often in projects: HR AI counts as high-risk almost always. Annex III point 4 explicitly names employment and personnel management — including AI-powered candidate selection, promotion decisions, and performance evaluation. Anyone enabling AI recruiting features in existing HR software is very likely a high-risk deployer as a result. That classification belongs before go-live, not after.

This guide covers the AI Act in detail — it doesn't replace legal advice or a complete regulatory review. Other relevant frameworks (GDPR, industry-specific requirements, product liability) are only mentioned here in passing; you'll find them fully covered in the Compliance Center of the product.

Frequently asked questions

Does the new deadline (December 2027) also apply to systems already running?

Yes — the postponement applies to all Annex III systems regardless of whether they're newly introduced or already in use. Anyone already prepared doesn't have to wait for the deadline and can comply earlier.

Is AI-powered recruiting automatically high-risk?

In the vast majority of cases, yes. Annex III explicitly names employment and personnel management as a high-risk area — even AI add-on features in existing HR software, like applicant screening, fall under it. Classification belongs before rollout, not after.

Does the AI Act affect me even outside the EU?

Yes, if the output of your system is used in the EU (market-location principle) — regardless of where the provider or operator is based.

Is the deadline until 2027 enough reason to do nothing right now?

Technically yes, practically no: conformity assessment, risk management, and documentation need several months of lead time for more complex systems — the extended deadline is a buffer, not a free pass.
